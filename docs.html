First-Come First-Serve

(FCFS)

FCFS CPU Scheduling algorithm is one of the simplest and most intuitive scheduling methods used in operating systems. It operates on the principle that processes are executed in the order they arrive in the ready queue.

Some important points

  • The process that arrives first is the first to be executed by the CPU.
  • Once a process starts execution, it continues until it completes its entire burst time or voluntarily yields the CPU.
  • FCFS is a non-preemptive scheduling algorithm, meaning that once a process begins execution, it cannot be interrupted until it finishes or gives up the CPU.

Shortest Job First

(SJF)

SJF is an algorithm in which the process having the smallest execution time is chosen for the next execution. It significantly reduces the average waiting time for other processes awaiting execution.

Some important points

  • It aims to minimize the average waiting time for processes in the queue by executing the shortest job next.
  • SJF is a non-preemptive scheduling algorithm, meaning that once a process begins execution, it cannot be interrupted until it finishes or gives up the CPU.
  • SJF scheduling may lead to a starvation problem with long turnaround times and requires the burst time of jobs to be predetermined, which can be challenging to estimate accurately.

Longest Job First

(LJF)

LJF is a CPU scheduling algorithm that prioritizes processes based on their burst time. In LJF, the processes with the largest burst time are given priority over the shorter ones.

Some important points

  • LJF is the opposite of the Shortest Job First (SJF) algorithm, where it focuses on executing the longest processes first, which can lead to longer waiting times for shorter processes.
  • LJF scheduling is not commonly used in practice due to its tendency to favor longer processes over shorter ones, which can result in inefficiencies and potential starvation for shorter jobs.
  • It is a non-preemptive scheduling algorithm, i.e. once a process begins execution, it cannot be interrupted until it finishes or gives up the CPU.

Round Robbin

(RR)

Round-robin scheduling is a CPU scheduling algorithm where time slices are assigned to each process in equal portions and in circular order, handling all processes without priority.

Some important points

  • It is a preemptive algorithm where each process is given a fixed time quantum to execute before being interrupted and moved to the back of the ready queue.
  • It is simple, easy-to-implement, and starvation-free algorithm that ensures fair CPU time allocation among processes by allowing each process to have a turn in a cyclic manner.
  • This algorithm ensures that no process monopolizes the CPU and that all processes get a fair share of CPU time.

Shortest Remaining

Job First

(SRJF)

Shortest Remaining Job First is the preemptive version of Shortest Job Next (SJN) algorithm, where the processor is allocated to the job closest to completion. SRJF prioritizes executing the process with the smallest remaining execution time.

Some important points

  • SRJF is a preemptive scheduling algorithm where the process with the smallest remaining execution time is selected for execution.
  • It aims to minimize the average waiting time by prioritizing the process with the shortest remaining time, leading to efficient CPU utilization.
  • SRJF scheduling can prevent longer processes from monopolizing the CPU, promoting fairness and responsiveness in process execution.

Preemptive Priority Scheduling

(PPS)

In this Scheduling, at the time of arrival of a process in the ready queue, its Priority is compared with the priority of the other processes present in the ready queue as well as with the one which is being executed by the CPU at that point of time. The One with the highest priority among all the available processes will be given the CPU next.

Some important points

  • Processes are assigned priority levels, and the CPU executes the process with the highest priority first. If a process with a higher priority arrives, it can preempt the currently running process.
  • One of the advantages of this Scheduling is its ability to prioritize critical tasks effectively, especially in real-time systems and multimedia applications.
  • A potential disadvantage of this algorithm is the risk of lower priority processes being starved if higher priority tasks continuously arrive, impacting fairness in process execution.
